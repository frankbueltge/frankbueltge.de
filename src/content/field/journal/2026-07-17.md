## Verify-before-propose (conductor's hand, first-hand)

Primary source: Yamada, Lange, Cong Lu, Chris Lu, Hu, Foerster, Ha, Clune, *Towards End-to-End
Automation of AI Research*, **Nature 651, 914–919 (2026)**; preprint arXiv:2606.15497 (arXiv
metadata records the Nature publication). The Nature page (s41586-026-10265-5) is paywalled; the
arXiv preprint carries the identical title and the Nature-published note. Extracted verbatim from
the preprint PDF (two independent extraction routes agreed):

> "We benchmarked the Automated Reviewer against human decisions using ICLR data from the publicly
> available OpenReview dataset (González-Márquez and Kobak, 2024). The Automated Reviewer achieves
> a comparable balanced accuracy with humans (69% versus 66%; see Supplementary Section A.3.2 for
> details) and a higher F1-score compared to inter-human group agreement (0.62 versus 0.49) in the
> NeurIPS 2021 consistency experiment (Beygelzimer et al., 2021), where roughly 10% of submissions
> were randomly selected and sent to two independent review committees, providing a real-world
> benchmark of inter-reviewer consistency (Table 1)."

- **Ground truth for the automated reviewer's "balanced accuracy" = ICLR accept/reject decisions**
  (OpenReview dataset). The human "66% / 0.49 F1" figure is the **NeurIPS 2021 inter-committee
  consistency** — a *different venue and a different quantity* (panel-vs-panel agreement, not
  accuracy against a label). The paper frames them as comparable ("replicate the collective
  judgment of human reviewers with high fidelity").
- Pipeline: an **ensemble of five reviews + a meta-review where the model acts as Area Chair.**
- Table 1 splits results by pre-cutoff (2017–2024) and post-cutoff (2025); BA 0.69 ± 0.04.
- Limitation confirmed verbatim: "Common failure modes include ... hallucinations such as
  inaccurate citations."
- The AI-generated paper that "passed peer review": ICLR 2025 ICBINB **workshop** (stated
  acceptance rate 70%), scores 6/7/6 (avg 6.33), withdrawn before publication as pre-committed.

**Context fetched first-hand to arm the roles:**
- The oracle's own arbitrariness (Beygelzimer et al., *Has the ML Review Process Become More
  Arbitrary...? The NeurIPS 2021 Consistency Experiment*, arXiv:2306.03262): the two committees
  disagreed on accept/reject for ~23% of papers; **~50% of papers accepted by the first committee
  were rejected by the second (50.6% in 2021; 49.5% in the 2014 replication).** "Substantial
  arbitrariness."
- **Novelty caution (prior-art scan):** the *thesis* that human accept/reject is a noisy ground
  truth for an LLM reviewer is already well-established in the 2025–2026 literature (e.g.
  arXiv:2605.03202 "Stop Automating Peer Review Without Rigorous Evaluation"; 2606.25057;
  2512.22145). As with "The Floor," any contribution must be a *specific instrument/move on the
  paper's own numbers*, not the thesis.

## The spike (conductor's hand — verify-before-build, sessions 12/16/27/39 precedent)

Ran the gate both roles demanded. Environment had no data libraries; installed a parquet reader and
fetched the pinned public dataset (`berenslab/iclr-dataset`, `data/iclr24v2.parquet`, 24,445 ICLR
submissions 2017–2024, sha256 `f486c7a0…d47fb0`, source URL recorded). Deterministic threshold sweep.
Full artifact + reproducible script: `notes/2026-07-17-automated-reviewer-spike/`.

**Result — GATE PASSED, decisively:** a **one-parameter threshold on the mean human review score
(≥ ~5.7) reaches balanced accuracy ≈ 0.88** against ICLR accept/reject on the full 2017–2024 corpus
(n=19,685 clearly-decided scored papers), stable at 0.87–0.91 per year; constant predictor 0.50
(the Proposer's correction confirmed). The paper's five-review-ensemble-plus-Area-Chair pipeline
reports **0.69** on the same ground truth; its human comparator (**0.66**) is the inter-committee
noise floor.

**The spike also sharpened the framing away from a naive headline** (the value of running it before
writing anything): the threshold uses the mean *human score*, which the paper's from-text reviewer
never sees — so this is **not** "trivial beats sophisticated." What it shows is that the ICLR
accept/reject label is **~88% recoverable from one number**, i.e. the decision is largely a
threshold on the scores; "balanced accuracy against the decision" is therefore mostly a
score-recovery task, and the tool is declared human-level by comparison to the **noise floor (0.66)**
rather than to the **signal (0.88)**. That input-asymmetry caveat, and the non-strict-replication
caveat (Supplementary A.3.2 unread), are load-bearing and recorded on the probe.
