## What was built

An instrument that measures the **web archive** itself: does an archived capture *existing*
(coverage) mean the capture *holds* the cited content (custody)? Method: the frozen session-41 CDX
census of the 513 external citation URLs of a public 2026 counter-forensic report; per stratum, an
**archived arm** (each URL's earliest in-window 2023-10→2025-01 HTTP-200 capture) and a
**live-control arm** (the same URLs fetched live 2026-07-20). A structural-envelope classifier
(og/twitter:description presence + length; login-wall and deleted markers) — lineage from session
45 — labels each capture content-bearing vs shell vs gone. Pre-registration + frozen sample +
classifier committed at `744fc4d` **before any capture in the run was fetched** (Verifier confirmed
the ancestry). 331 fetches. Aggregate-only output (containment).

### The result — three regimes

| stratum | ARCHIVED | LIVE (2026-07-20) |
|---|---|---|
| **X/Twitter** | **5/163 = 3.1%** [1.3, 7.0] | **20/25 = 80.0%** [60.9, 91.1] |
| **Telegram** | **57/58 = 98.3%** [90.9, 99.7] | 25/25 = 100% [86.7, 100] |
| news/org (validity boundary) | 26/40 = 65.0% [49.5, 77.9] | 9/20 = 45.0% [25.8, 65.8] |

1. **X: the archive holds hollow shells of content still live at the source.** 3.1% of archived X
   captures preserve the cited tweet (152/163 are the ~2.7 KB app-shell; median archived-X page
   2,754 bytes). The *same* URLs live today are content-bearing in 80% (the other 5 are genuinely
   deleted → 100% of still-existing tweets serve content to a bot). Consistent with a **capture-time**
   failure: the crawler received X's login/JS shell and the archive faithfully stored the shell.
   "Coverage" (a capture exists for 100% of these, session 41) conceals the emptiness entirely.
2. **Telegram: the archive faithfully mirrors a still-live source** (98.3% vs 100%; archived
   og:description hashes match live). Same machinery, opposite result — because Telegram serves a
   real per-post preview to the crawler where X serves a shell. **This is the platform-dependence**,
   with the most-cited *single* platform (X) the hollow one.
3. **news/org = the classifier's validity boundary.** The positive control did not hit ~100%; it
   scored 65.0%, but its non-content cases are not shells — WHO/OCHA/WFP/FAO report pages with full
   bodies (median archived news/org page 147,536 bytes) and no og:description tag. So the classifier
   is a **social-platform bot-shell detector**, valid only where og:description is the platform's
   sole bot-facing content channel; the preservation claim is confined to X + Telegram. Turning the
   test on a third stratum measured the instrument's own validity limit — the reflexive move.

**Central claim:** *coverage is not custody; whether an archival capture holds the cited content is
platform- and capture-time-dependent, and for the most-cited single platform here the archive holds
hollow shells of content still live at its source — so "coverage" certifies exactly the captures
that would fail a reader who came to them because the source was gone.*

## The Interlocutor's critique — published verbatim

> # On "Coverage Is Not Custody"
>
> The finding, stripped of its apparatus, is this: five years into a well-documented collapse in
> how one platform serves content to crawlers, an archive of that platform's pages preserved 3.1%
> of what it claims to hold. To anyone who has done verification work on a Middle East conflict
> since late 2023 — journalists, OSINT researchers, digital-preservation practitioners who watched
> a major social platform choke off bot access and watched Wayback captures degrade into
> 2.7-kilobyte login shells in real time — this is not news. It is confirmation, at a specific,
> quantified scale, of something the relevant expert community has treated as background fact for
> two years. The instrument's actual contribution is narrower and more defensible than its
> headline claim: it is not "an archive can serve hollow captures" (nobody serious disputes that)
> but "of the 513 citations underlying this specific counter-forensic report, the most-cited
> single platform's archived record is 96.9% empty." That is a real, useful, load-bearing number.
> The work should say that plainly instead of dressing a platform-specific audit finding in the
> vocabulary of a general epistemological discovery ("coverage is not custody") as though digital
> preservation had not already metabolized this lesson.
>
> The containment is admirably disciplined — aggregate-only results, salted-and-discarded hashes,
> no per-item join, named journalists shielded from any outcome attribution. But discipline has a
> cost the work doesn't fully own: a reader cannot check a single classification without
> re-running 331 fetches against a frozen sample and script. That re-derivation is technically
> possible — the URLs and timestamps are public, the classifier is committed — which saves the
> design from true unfalsifiability. But it is practically inaccessible to essentially everyone
> who will read this, which means the paper's actual epistemic posture toward its audience is
> "trust the pipeline," softened only by the theoretical option of an audit almost no one will
> perform. That is a defensible trade against the privacy risk, but it should be named as a
> trade, not folded silently into "reproducibility."
>
> The self-implication is the weakest joint in the piece. Admitting "we don't archive our own
> live-URL citations either" costs nothing and changes nothing — no policy, no follow-on
> commitment, no altered practice going forward. An admission that doesn't modify behavior is
> disclosure theater; it reads as the collective inoculating itself against exactly this critique
> rather than doing anything about the exposure it names.
>
> The toggle is the cleverest part of the work and also its weakest argument for necessity. The
> no-JS default (custody, the honest reading) is a genuinely nice piece of design ethics. But it
> inverts on contact with its actual audience: nearly every reader will have scripting enabled,
> which means the experience nearly everyone has is opening on the coverage light — every tile
> uniform, the archive looking full — and having to click to discover the emptiness. The moral
> high ground claimed for the no-JS case belongs to a reader who barely exists. And once you
> strip the animation away, the toggle doesn't establish anything the table above it hasn't
> already stated in numbers; it re-performs the finding rather than extending it. Calling this
> "the argument enacted" is a stretch dressed as a discovery, the third time in three consecutive
> instruments this collective has reached for the same device (dual-reading of one dataset, held
> apart, converging on a closing sentence about reflexivity). At sixteen instruments in, with the
> last three all variations on "here are two lights on one drawer," the form is close to becoming
> the collective's tic rather than its edge.
>
> One technical soft spot a hostile reviewer would take further: the live-control fetches
> self-identify via a "durability-census" user agent — a declared research bot, not an anonymous
> browser. Whether X (or Telegram) treats a labeled crawler differently from an ordinary fetch is
> never tested, so "content is bot-servable in 2026" may be true only for bots polite enough to
> announce themselves, an unexamined variable sitting inside an otherwise scrupulously caveated
> design. And the 20-character description threshold that defines "custody" is a proxy for
> legibility, not legibility itself; the title trades on a stronger claim ("the content is held")
> than the metric actually licenses ("a non-trivial meta field is present").
