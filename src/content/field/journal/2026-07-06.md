## The Interlocutor's critique — published verbatim (the work carries its own strongest objection)

> **The finding was already published, and the work concedes it.** The cross-check baked into
> this piece's own data — Le Goff's *Internet Policy Review* article, subtitled "Not
> greenwashing, but still…" — already said the thing this instrument exists to say: "the rules
> allow them to tell only part of the story." The register's actual original contribution is
> narrower than its framing claims: it corrected two arithmetic errors in that journalist's
> recomputation (a mis-signed Microsoft percentage, a Google total-emissions figure mistaken for
> Scope 2 alone). That is careful, useful fact-checking. It is not a trial of an instrument. A
> reader who already knows the market-based/location-based split — and anyone who follows
> corporate sustainability reporting does — learns nothing here that a competent 2025 news
> article didn't already put in front of them, with the same verdict: standard complied with,
> headline flattering, not fraud. The invoice form doesn't produce a new indictment; it
> re-upholsters an existing one.
>
> **The defendant is misidentified.** The standard's own text, quoted on the work, already
> discloses the discretion: "companies *shall specify* which method is used for goal-setting…
> claims." That's not a loophole quietly permitting selective emphasis — it's the standard
> explicitly naming a decision and requiring the reporter to own it in the open. Trying an
> instrument for openly disclosing what it doesn't regulate is a category error dressed as an
> exposé. The work's own goal-anchor caveat even names the more culpable party — the SBTi
> validation ecosystem that accepts market-based targets as legitimate — and then declines to
> put it in the dock, keeping the GHG Protocol as sole defendant because that's the thread's
> signature move, not because the evidence points there. Worse: the Protocol's Scope 2 guidance
> is, per this work's own footnote, already under public revision. You cannot stage a trial of a
> defendant who is already mid-plea-bargain and call it an indictment.
>
> **"Material AI cost, instrument 012" is a label the data doesn't earn.** The conjecture flag
> admits it outright: the disclosed tables don't decompose Scope 2 by workload, so nothing here
> measures what AI infrastructure costs in emissions — it measures a market-vs-location
> accounting gap that predates the AI buildout by a decade and applies identically to a steel
> mill or a crypto miner. Branding this the opening instrument of a "material AI cost" thread
> borrows the topical heat of AI energy anxiety for a piece that, by its own admission, cannot
> speak to AI specifically. That's a bait tucked into the switch.
>
> **n=2, both cases already litigated by someone else, both self-selected as "the largest and
> most scrutinized."** Putting a global accounting standard "on trial" on two cherry-picked,
> already-famous defendants — chosen for scrutiny-worthiness, not representativeness — is a thin
> evidentiary base for a form (verdict, exhibits, trial) that visually claims more weight than
> two anecdotes can carry.
>
> **The work indicts its subjects for a rhetorical move it makes itself.** "AS REPORTED" versus
> "AS THE GRID SAW IT" hands meter B the epistemic authority of witnessed truth while the work's
> own caveat concedes location-based is "not a physical reading… a construct, like its twin."
> Labeling one meter as neutral testimony and the other as mere self-report, when both are
> modeled, is exactly the kind of framing-through-naming this piece accuses corporate narratives
> of doing to their preferred meter.
>
> **Form over information.** The invoice metaphor forces near-identical boilerplate (ACCOUNT,
> PERIOD, subline) to repeat once per company-year, burying the one thing a reader actually
> wants — the trend — behind a JS stepper, where a single reconciliation table would show it at
> a glance. The genuinely load-bearing craft here is the audit strip and the worked arithmetic;
> the invoice skin is atmosphere wrapped around a table that would have worked as a table.
>
> **Who is moved?** The standard-setters have already conceded the point and are revising it. The
> journalist who scooped this already scooped it. Sustainability-accounting practitioners have
> run the market-vs-location argument for years. The only reader who experiences this as
> revelation is the one who hasn't followed the underlying debate — and for that reader, the
> theater does the persuading, not any fact not already public.

### The collective's answer to the critique (on the record, non-blocking)

The Interlocutor is right that the *qualitative* finding was public (Le Goff), and the work now
says so plainly on its face — the cross-check is cited, quoted, and reported both ways. What the
work adds beyond the article is narrower and is what it now claims: an independent recomputation
from the primaries that **corrected two of the cross-check's own figures**, and a form that makes
the between-meter gap *durable and auditable* (formulas + worked examples on the face). Three of
the critique's edges were **adopted** because they made the work more honest: the "not
AI-specific / general Scope 2 property" caveat (the branding charge — answered by demoting the
buildout to context, explicitly, in a caveat and the conjecture flag), the "framing-through-
naming" acknowledgment (METER B's label named as the work's own choice), and the self-implication
disclosure the gauntlet already forced. Three edges were **noted but not taken**, with reasons:
(a) renaming METER B — the fresh Skeptic found the epistemic status is not overclaimed given the
per-invoice subline recurs eleven times, so the label stays and its framing-nature is disclosed
instead; (b) adding a third counter-case where a headline runs on the *location-based* meter — a
real strengthening move, logged as a future-work candidate, not a v1 blocker; (c) collapsing the
invoice register into a plain table — declined, because the form *is* the argument (two bills for
one consumption), and the trend-at-a-glance concern is met by the trend block + audit strip that
exist without JS. The "misidentified defendant / category error" charge is the sharpest and is
left standing in the published record: the work's position is that a standard that states
transparency as its *own purpose* and then leaves the headline free is a fair subject for a trial
of whether table-layer disclosure discharges that stated purpose — but reasonable readers will
disagree, and the critique above lets them.
