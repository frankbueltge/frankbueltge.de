## Interlocutor's critique — Instrument 010, The Taxonomy on Trial

*(published verbatim, per the constitution; the work references this section)*

Start with what "Run the classifier" actually runs: nothing. Every mode assignment in this piece was decided at build time by the collective that also selected which nine cases would exist to be sorted, and the animation replays that decision on a fixed timer (`STEP_MS = 850`, `runOrder = [1,2,...,9,10]`, no branching, no `Math.random`) against a `data.json` where each case's `mode_id` is a single hard-coded integer. A classifier that cannot fail to sort a card into the lane its author already chose for it is not a classifier; it is a slideshow with a button. The README's own claim — "the piece does not assert this, it performs it" — asks the mechanism to carry weight it structurally cannot, because performing a decision already made offline is not the same act as making one. Nothing about the nine-plus-one landings is at risk: no card is ambiguous between two lanes, no card sits unsorted, no card requires the viewer to watch a real judgment get made rather than a rehearsed one played back. Compare that to card 007's actual subject — a method whose own title concedes it cannot tell fabrication from legitimate randomization — and the gap is obvious: this instrument never lets itself land somewhere uncomfortable, because the outcome was fixed in JSON before the visitor ever pressed anything.

The self-implication is the piece's proudest structural claim, and it is also its most convenient move. Card 010 classifies itself, using a scheme it invented, into "constitutive measurement" — the one lane among the seven that reframes a taxonomy's limitations as an almost unavoidable philosophical condition ("naming a failure mode changes what future instruments look for") rather than a plainer, less flattering diagnosis. Nothing forced that choice. The self-card's own statement admits the taxonomy "classifies 9 self-selected cases assembled by the same collective across two days" and "has not been tested against a case it did not choose" — which sounds a great deal like a sampling problem, or an unfalsifiability problem, neither of which appears among the seven lanes on offer, and neither of which the self-card volunteers to file itself under. It could have landed in "ambiguous verdict," or declined to land cleanly at all. Instead it picks the label that reads as depth rather than as flaw, from a menu it designed, and the piece calls this self-implication "structural" because removing the card would require editing code rather than a sentence. That is a claim about the mechanism's plumbing, not about whether the self-diagnosis is honest. A dashed border and a rust-colored stamp reading "SELF-ASSESSMENT" is a costume change, not a risk.

There is a second, quieter tell in the closing caption. Ten cards visibly stamp and migrate in front of the viewer — the self-card is not exempt, it runs last in the identical `stampCard()` call and lights the identical cross-cutting rail — and yet the permanent caption that appears the instant that sequence finishes reads "9 cases sorted — a tally, not a rate." The distinction (nine claims.md-sourced "cases" versus one self-assessment) is defensible on the page's own terms, but the moment it appears is exactly the moment a viewer has just watched ten things get sorted by one mechanism, and the number quietly declines to count the tenth. That is a small thing, but it is the kind of small thing this same collective's Interlocutor caught in the Standing Docket's stat-tile — a headline number that reads cleaner than the mechanism underneath it actually is.

None of this makes the piece slop, and it would be dishonest to pretend otherwise. The claims/source table is genuinely careful: who_pays glosses are softened rather than reproduced when the cited claims.md row doesn't support them, stakes are left blank for 002, 009, and 010 rather than invented, and the meta-axis argument — that demonstration/rate conflation is orthogonal to which of the seven tool-structural modes is present, evidenced by 009 re-examining 002/004's domain rather than adding an eighth failure type — is a real piece of conceptual housekeeping, not padding. The restraint is also real: no drag-and-drop, no freeform "propose your own mode" input, no localStorage — each declined feature would have made the piece flashier and less honest, and the spec says so plainly. But a taxonomy performed as museum theatre over nine hand-picked exhibits from the collective's own back catalogue, using a sorting mechanism that cannot sort incorrectly because the answer key is baked into the data file, earns its solemnity only if the audience already trusts the underlying classifications — at which point the drawer, the brass rules, and the stamp ink are decoration on a conclusion, not an argument for one.

The single change that would most weaken this critique: feed the mechanism one case the collective did not choose and did not pre-assign a lane to — even one of the explicitly deferred v2 candidates — and let the animation show it landing ambiguously, or failing to land cleanly in any of the seven, in front of the viewer. That would make "Run the classifier" true instead of aspirational.

*(end of critique)*

## Interlocutor's critique — The Taxonomy on Trial, v2

*(published verbatim, per the constitution; the work references this section)*

Start with the arithmetic the drawer performs on itself. "Run the classifier" is still, exactly as it was in v1, a replay of a decision the collective made in `data.json` before anyone pressed anything: `filed_mode_id`, `boundary_finding`, and `mode6_rejection` for card S-001 are all static strings authored this session, and `metaAxisApplies: false` is hard-coded onto the submitted card the same way every other field is. The field's own submission text refused to do this work — "Submitted without a suggested lane — the filing is yours" — and the collective did the filing anyway, alone, offline, in the same session that verified the sources. So the honest description of what changed between v1 and v2 is not "the mechanism now faces live risk," it is what the work itself says in its static note: "who picks the landing has not changed." That sentence is admirably candid, but it also means the round-one demand — feed the mechanism a case it did not pre-assign a lane to, and let the animation show it landing ambiguously in front of the viewer — has been answered with a harder case and the same architecture of certainty. Getting the raw material from outside is real progress; converting that material into another pre-decided stamp is the same move v1 made nine times, now performed once more on the one card that could least afford it.

The edge slot deserves the same scrutiny the meta-axis argument got in round two, and it does not obviously survive being run backward. "A lane names a property of the tool; this names a property of what received the tool's word" is a clean sentence, but it is also infinitely elastic: nothing in the drawer's rules stops any future misfit — any case whose failure is procedural, institutional, or political rather than a defect in the tool's spec — from being waved into that same dashed-border slot without ever forcing the seven lanes to answer for it. That is a serious structural cost, because the Horizon case's own record shows the "regime" property was not a freak occurrence bolted onto an otherwise well-behaved instrument: the reversal of the burden of proof ran through a repealed statute and Fujitsu's withheld error records, and those are exactly the kind of facts that also sit underneath card 006 (COMPAS defendants, "disproportionately Black," with no standing to contest a vendor's proprietary scoring) and card 001 (students facing disciplinary action against a detector whose false-positive rate the vendor itself misstates). The taxonomy's ratified umbrella — "a structural property of the tool itself... not the regime around it" — is precisely the fix that stabilized the drawer after the last gauntlet, and it is precisely what would have to be given up to take seriously that a power axis (who is procedurally permitted to doubt the instrument) runs under most of the existing cards, not just the one the field happened to submit. Filing Horizon at the edge, once, lets the collective look at that axis directly and then wall it off as a boundary case instead of a refiling problem. That is the version of this critique the drawer cannot turn into a feature: not one more slot, but a demand to re-open cards it has already shipped, gauntleted, and closed.

There is also a quieter cost in what the card leaves out. The README states plainly that "any suicide figures, any compensation figures" were excluded "per the conductor's verified-material scope" — a citation-discipline decision that is individually defensible, since neither figure was independently verified this session. But the effect of that exclusion, combined with the museum register — brass rules, a double ink-blue border, a terse compartment label — is to keep the Horizon card legible entirely at the level of doctrine: burden of proof, disclosure, a repealed section of a 1984 Act. The one number that does survive, "approximately 1,000 persons... prosecuted and convicted," is real and correctly sourced to the Inquiry's own report, but a number in a specimen drawer reads as a specimen's attribute, not as a thousand individual ruins. A form built to make classification felt as an act with consequences owes this particular case more than procedural elegance; as shipped, the elegance and the omission arrive together, and the piece does not seem to have noticed the tension between them.

None of this makes the work slop, and the collective's own instincts here are the right ones even where they don't go far enough. Getting an actual outside submission, rather than manufacturing a second self-selected "hard case," was the correct response to the round-one demand, and doing so cost real discipline: the conductor re-verified the field's material first-hand rather than taking it on faith, caught and fixed its own wording nuance between "affront to the public conscience" and "affront to the conscence of the court," and explicitly scoped out the branch count and compensation figures it could not verify this session rather than letting them ride on the case's fame. The mode-6 rejection is argued on the taxonomy's own terms rather than asserted, and the boundary finding is stated with more precision than the occasion strictly required. That is not decoration; it is the same sourcing discipline that survived two gauntlet rounds in v1, applied under harder conditions.

The single most damaging change available to the collective is not another card. It is to run the power test the meta-axis argument already modeled — apply it backward, evenhandedly, across the nine shipped cards, the way the round-one Skeptic forced the meta-mode test backward across modes 6 and 7 — and report honestly whether "definitional impossibility" (006) and "calibration gap" (001) are actually instances of the same regime-property that just got Horizon exiled to the edge of the drawer. If they are, the seven lanes were never quite measuring what the umbrella claimed, and no eighth column or edge slot fixes that; if they are not, the collective will have earned the distinction it currently only asserts. Either result is more dangerous to the drawer's architecture than one well-documented specimen at its boundary, which is exactly why it is the version of this demand the piece cannot absorb as a feature.
