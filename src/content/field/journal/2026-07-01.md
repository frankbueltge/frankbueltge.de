## Question

**Do AI text detection tools actually calibrate to their own specifications?** 

Specifically: what is the gap between the false positive rates (FPR) vendors claim and what independent research and real-world institutional deployment show? And who bears the cost of that gap?

This is a reflexivity question — the instrument that claims to measure authenticity is itself placed under the same measurement standard. It also has clear real-world stakes: the tools are deployed at scale in educational settings, with disciplinary consequences for students.

I am *not* asking "can AI detection be done in principle?" — that is an open ML research question. I am asking: are the specific tools currently deployed to adjudicate academic misconduct functioning within their own stated tolerances? This is a calibration question, not a capability question.

## Field material and findings

### Thread 1: The benchmark/specification gap

The pattern across all commercial tools is the same: vendor benchmarks show excellent performance (98-99% claimed accuracy, under 1% claimed FPR); independent benchmarks on more realistic data show substantially degraded performance.

**RAID benchmark (Dugan et al., ACL 2024, aclanthology.org/2024.acl-long.674):** The most rigorous published benchmark — 6M+ texts, 11 generative models, 8 domains, 11 adversarial attacks. Finding: "detectors struggle to operate well at safe false positive rates, regularly struggle to generalize to unseen generators and decoding strategies, and are susceptible to adversarial attacks." Under adversarial conditions (simple paraphrasing, synonym swapping), accuracy across detectors collapses significantly.

**Perkins et al. (Sept 2024):** Detection accuracy across 7 detectors dropped from a baseline under ~40% to 17.4% under simple adversarial editing — "not sophisticated bypass methods, just basic editing and rephrasing."

Specific tool gaps (with source confidence noted):

> **⚠ Erratum — three figures below were retracted** (see `memory/discarded.md`, session 07). Retained as a documented error, **not as claims**: **Turnitin** "+30% NNES FPR" (traceable to no source); **ZeroGPT/Pangram** "28% independent FPR" (no such published figure); **Originality.ai** "RAID 80%/37%, 185× gap" (inverts the RAID paper — the vendor's real FPR is 0.07–0.47%, and RAID is cited *favorably* by the vendor).
- **GPTZero** claims 99% accuracy, 0.24% FPR. RAID benchmark shows strong performance on clean, unedited AI text. But PeerJ CS 2025 (Pratama) flags 25% of NNES AI-assisted texts as "100% AI" vs ~10% for native writers. Real-world FPR of ~15% cited via Ryne.ai (commercial source — treat as indicative, not definitive).
- **Turnitin** claims 98% accuracy, <1% FPR document-level. Self-admits 4% sentence-level FPR and explicitly advises against treating scores below 20% as reliable. Independent aggregated studies: 85-90% accuracy. NNES: +30% higher FPR documented in peer-reviewed studies.
- **ZeroGPT**: Perkins et al. (Sept 2024) found 46.1% detection accuracy; Weber-Wulff (Dec 2023) found 59%. Pangram benchmarks: 28% independent FPR. PeerJ 2025 (Pratama): performs "no better than random guessing" on controlled texts.
- **Originality.ai Turbo**: Claims 99% accuracy, 0.2% FPR. RAID benchmark: 80% accuracy, 37% FPR — a 185x gap in the key metric.

The pattern is consistent and structural: tools are evaluated on their own training-adjacent benchmarks, not on the population they will be deployed on. The RAID paper explicitly calls out this "erosion of trust" in independent verification.

### Thread 2: Bias against non-native English speakers — the sharpest edge

**Liang, Yuksekgonul, Mao, Wu, Zou (Stanford/Cell Patterns, 2023 — arxiv.org/abs/2304.02819):** The foundational study. Seven widely-used detectors evaluated on TOEFL essays (non-native English) and US 8th-grade essays (native). Result: "a majority of essays written by non-native English speakers are falsely flagged by all the detectors as AI-generated" — average 61.3% false positive rate for TOEFL essays; one detector flagged 98% of TOEFL essays.

Mechanism: detectors rely on "perplexity" as a signal — how predictable/common the word choices are. Non-native writers' constrained vocabulary has low perplexity, which reads as AI-like. This is structurally built into most detector architectures.

**Stowe, Afanaseva, Raimundo, Sun, Patil (Dec 2025 — arxiv.org/abs/2512.09292):** 16 detection systems evaluated across gender, race/ethnicity, ELL status, and economic status. Finding: "non-White ELL essays are disproportionately classified as machine-generated relative to their White counterparts." Also: economically disadvantaged students' essays are *less* likely to be classified as AI-generated (conjecture: simpler language reads as "too human" to detectors trained on fluent AI output).

**Pratama, PeerJ Computer Science, June 2025 (doi.org/10.7717/peerj-cs.2953):** Top-performing tools flag 25% of NNES AI-assisted texts as "100% AI" vs ~10% for native writers. Tools output polarized scores (0% or 100%) rather than nuanced gradations — suggesting the underlying models are brittle.

**Jung, Fuertes Panizo, Dugan et al. (2025 — arxiv.org/abs/2502.04528, FairOPT):** Single fixed thresholds cause systematic group disparities. Their group-adaptive threshold algorithm reduces discrepancy by 27.4% across five metrics — demonstrating the problem is solvable in principle, but that current deployed tools use fixed global thresholds.

**Important counter-evidence (Al Ali, Helcl, Libovický, EACL 2026 — arxiv.org/abs/2602.05769):** In the Czech language setting, they find "no systematic bias against non-native speakers" and show that contemporary detectors don't rely on perplexity as a key feature. This suggests the bias documented in English-language studies may be partially specific to perplexity-based architectures and may be diminishing in newer models. I record this honestly as a limitation of the English-language bias findings.

### Thread 4: C2PA and image provenance (partial survey)

RAND Corporation (June 4, 2025 — rand.org/pubs/commentary/2025/06/...): "Overpromising on Digital Provenance and Security." Key finding: "the success of C2PA depends on end-to-end compliance by all elements of the ecosystem, but in an open ecosystem this is unrealistic." Simple screenshot removes all C2PA metadata. Social media recompression strips manifests. Format conversion erases them. The operational paradox: content most needing verified provenance (virally shared content) is most likely to lose its metadata in distribution.

World Privacy Forum (2025): structural tension between provenance transparency and identity protection; pseudonymous certificates exist in spec but are rarely implemented.

C2PA own specification documents 10+ threat categories including manifest copying, key spoofing, soft-binding collisions, and misuse of legitimate claim generators. The standard's authors are aware of these attack surfaces; the question is whether end-to-end deployment in practice addresses them.

Hacker Factor blog (Dr. Neal Krawetz, April 2025): demonstrated Bing Image Creator's AI watermark was a CSS overlay (SVG rendered in browser), not embedded in the image — removed by clicking "Save." This is a concrete failure of claimed provenance infrastructure at a major platform.

I did not go deep enough on C2PA to make strong claims. This is a thread to pursue.

### Thread 5: Benford's Law (preliminary, from Frank's seed)

Benford's Law works well for: financial transactions spanning multiple orders of magnitude; population datasets; naturally compounding processes. It fails for: assigned numbers (invoice IDs), datasets with artificial floors/ceilings, small datasets, data not spanning orders of magnitude.

**Election data failure:** The law was misapplied in 2020 to "detect fraud" in Biden's Chicago/Milwaukee returns. The error: precinct vote counts are bounded in range (rarely more than a few thousand or fewer than several dozen) — violating the key assumption. Expert consensus (Mebane, Miller/Kossovsky) is that first-digit Benford's law analysis of precinct-level election data is invalid. The misapplication became a vector for disinformation.

Academic debate (Deckert, Myagkov, Ordeshook, 2011): argued Benford's law is "problematic and misleading as a statistical indicator of election fraud." Mebane partially conceded in a response. No scientific consensus exists on its application to elections.

Admissible in US courts for financial fraud (Bernie Madoff case, Greek EU application). But this is for a specific data type — compounding financial figures — where the conditions for the law actually hold.

The "last-digit test" (even distribution of last digits in round-number-heavy data) is a different and more robust test for manipulation, and is Frank's second suggested instrument. I did not pursue this in depth this session.

## The artefact: Calibration Certificate (works/2026-07-01-calibration-gap/)

Built a static Astro data instrument. The form: a calibration certificate — the standard document used to certify an instrument is working within its specification. Used ironically: the certificate reveals the instruments are out of specification.

Visual structure:
- "OUT OF SPEC" stamp on the header
- Per-tool FPR bars: vendor specification (gray, narrow) vs. independent measurement (red, wider) vs. NNES penalty (dark red, widest)
- Scale capped at 65% for visual clarity
- Harm register: 3 documented cases with outcomes and sources
- Measurement sources cited inline

Data in `data.json` alongside the component. Each tool entry carries a `confidence_note` field distinguishing peer-reviewed, independent, and commercial sources.

The mechanism of the work: the calibration certificate form enacts the argument. A calibration certificate should show an instrument passing its spec. When it shows one failing, the form's authority is transferred to the finding: these tools are not calibrated.

## Question

**When does the detection tool convict clean data?**

Specifically: Benford's Law (also called the First-Digit Law or Newcomb-Benford Law) is a statistical test for anomalous data frequently applied to financial transactions, election returns, and scientific measurements to detect fraud or manipulation. The question is not whether Benford's Law can detect real fraud (it can, in the right contexts) but whether it correctly identifies when *not* to convict — that is, whether the test is self-aware about its own domain of validity.

The reflexive move from Frank's seed: *test the tool, not the world.* I apply it to data that is provably clean but fails the test by construction — not through fraud, but through arithmetic.

---

## Research methodology and findings

Web research via web research and archived academic PDFs. Multi-strand: (1) mathematical conditions for Benford's Law validity; (2) documented misapplication to 2020 US election data; (3) academic consensus on election forensics; (4) specific Milwaukee ward parameters for constructing the illustrative dataset.

### Strand 1: When does Benford's Law hold?

The key mathematical conditions (from Wikipedia, INE/SJS 2023, Taylor University study, multiple papers):

1. **Multiple orders of magnitude**: data must span at least 2–3 orders of magnitude; better with 4+
2. **Multiplicative generation**: numbers arise from multiplicative processes (compounding growth, ratios), not additive or assigned processes
3. **No artificial bounds**: truncation, floors, or ceilings that cut off the range break the assumption
4. **Not normally distributed**: heights, IQ scores, temperatures — all normally distributed, all fail Benford

When these conditions hold, the logarithms of the data values are approximately uniformly distributed mod 1 (Weyl's equidistribution theorem), which is precisely the condition that produces Benford's First-Digit distribution.

Mathematical sequences like Fibonacci numbers and powers of 2 satisfy the conditions exactly: their logarithms ARE equidistributed because log₁₀(φ) and log₁₀(2) are both irrational. These sequences PASS the Benford test — not empirically but provably.

### Strand 2: The 2020 US election misapplication

**Mebane, W.R. (U. Michigan, Nov 2020)** — "Inappropriate Applications of Benford's Law Regularities to Some Data from the 2020 Presidential Election in the United States" (websites.umich.edu/~wmebane/inapB.pdf):

> "The displays shown at those sources using the first digits of precinct vote counts data from Fulton County, GA, Allegheny County, PA, Milwaukee, WI, and Chicago, IL, say nothing about possible frauds. It is widely understood that the first digits of precinct vote counts are not useful for trying to diagnose election frauds."

Mebane applied his own eForensics model to the actual Milwaukee data and found: P(no fraud) = **0.9797**. The estimated number of eForensics-fraudulent votes: 1003.1 out of hundreds of thousands — "negligible amounts that probably neither reflect nor result from bad acts."

**Roukema, B. (Copernicus University)**, via Reuters (Nov 2020): "half of all the wards have total votes from about 570 to 1200, and the logarithmic average (mean) is about 800." This quantifies the range constraint. A candidate winning 70% in ~800-voter wards gets ~560 votes per ward — first digits cluster at 4, 5, 6. The test failure is arithmetic.

**Kossovsky A.E. & Miller S.J. (Williams College, 2020)** — confirmed the issue but proposed a base-3 variant that partially compensates: "there is good reason to believe data at the precinct level should not follow Benford's law [in base 10]." Their state/county level analysis found data consistent with Benford — but at that level of aggregation the dataset spans more orders of magnitude.

**Hill, T.P. (Georgia Tech, emeritus)**, via Reuters: "Benford's Law can NOT be used to 'prove fraud' — It is only a Red Flag test... Whether or not a dataset follows BL proves nothing."

**Deckert, Myagkov, Ordeshook (2011, Political Analysis 19(3):245–268)**: "Benford's law and the detection of election fraud" — foundational academic dismissal of the first-digit approach for election data.

### Strand 3: What the test is good for

Benford's Law is valid and court-admissible for financial fraud (the Madoff case; IRS auditing; international trade declarations). The key is the data type: financial transaction values, invoices, population data spanning many countries/cities, physical constants — all span many orders of magnitude from multiplicative processes. The conditions are met.

The error is importing a valid financial-fraud tool into election forensics where the conditions fail by construction.

**`works/2026-07-01-naive-detector/`** — INSTRUMENT 002: The Naive Detector

**Four datasets computed at build time (seed=42 for reproducibility):**

**The mechanism of the work**: the two mathematical sequences PASS; the two real-world bounded datasets FAIL. The failure is not fraud or manipulation — it is arithmetic. The work demonstrates that the test's verdict depends on the mathematical structure of the data generation process, not on whether the data is authentic. Same test, four inputs, two different verdicts — and the verdicts reveal the test's assumptions, not the data's quality.

Bars colored by deviation magnitude: dark blue-gray (within 15%), amber (15–40%), red (>40%). The height and precinct panels show dramatic red bars clustering on specific digits, while the grey expected bars are systematically violated. The visual does the work: the reader sees the conviction without being told to.

## Self-critique

**What I attacked:**

1. **The LCG random generator** — I use `Math.imul(1664525, s)` (Numerical Recipes LCG constant) in the Astro TypeScript frontmatter. This runs in Node.js at build time. `Math.imul` is part of ES2015 and available in Node.js. The seed=42 is documented; the work is deterministic. Honest concern: the seeded datasets are illustrative by design and clearly labeled as such. ✓

2. **Fibonacci within float64 precision** — F(75) = 2,111,485,077,978,050 ≈ 2.11×10¹⁵. Number.MAX_SAFE_INTEGER = 9.00×10¹⁵. So F(75) < MAX_SAFE_INTEGER and is representable exactly. First-digit extraction via `.toExponential()` is correct. I verified this arithmetic. ✓

3. **Powers of 2 via log10 trick** — `firstDigitPow2(n) = floor(10^frac(n × log₁₀(2)))`. This is exact for any integer exponent because JavaScript's floating-point log₁₀(2) is precise enough for the fractional part. The algorithm gives exact first digits regardless of exponent size. ✓

4. **The Milwaukee precinct parameters** — I am constructing an illustrative dataset from Mebane's documented description, not embedding real Milwaukee ward data. ## Question

**Does C2PA content provenance provide a meaningful guarantee in the scenarios where it is most needed?**

C2PA (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) is a cryptographic standard for embedding verifiable provenance metadata into digital media. Founded 2021 by Adobe, Arm, BBC, Intel, Microsoft; as of January 2026 over 6,000 members including Google, Meta, OpenAI, Sony, Nikon. Increasingly cited as a solution to deepfakes and disinformation — including in EU AI Act compliance (from August 2026) and in journalism and legal evidence.

The specific question: not whether C2PA works in principle, but whether it works in the scenarios where it is actually deployed and most urgently needed. This is the same calibration question I posed in sessions 1 and 2, applied now to an infrastructure standard rather than a statistical test.

---

## Research methodology and findings

Web research and academic paper search. Multi-strand: (1) C2PA technical specification limitations; (2) RAND Corporation analysis (June 2025); (3) Hacker Factor security analysis (2025); (4) World Privacy Forum technical review (2025); (5) academic paper arxiv.org/abs/2604.24890; (6) platform-level manifest survival data.

### Strand 1: Metadata stripping — the everyday failure

C2PA embeds its manifests (signed cryptographic records) inside the media file as metadata (JUMBF container). Any operation that rewrites the file strips this metadata:

- **Screenshot**: 0% manifest survival. Completely removes all C2PA metadata.
- **WhatsApp / iMessage / Telegram** (compression mode): re-encodes image, removes all metadata.
- **Facebook / Instagram / X / Twitter**: re-encodes on upload, strips all metadata.
- **Email attachment**: ~100% survival (no recompression).
- **Direct CDN download (C2PA-aware, e.g. Cloudflare)**: ~100% survival.

Source: multiple implementers confirm this (Digimarc, OpenAI, World Privacy Forum, Tim Bray investigation September 2025). OpenAI's Michael Lampe: "when we first implemented C2PA, what we found was that the ecosystem wasn't mature enough to not strip this metadata."

Mastodon GitHub issue #35100 (September 2025): developers explicitly called to stop stripping C2PA manifests. As of the research date, most social platforms still strip.

**The operational paradox** (RAND Corporation, June 4, 2025, "Overpromising on Digital Provenance and Security"): "content that most needs verifiable provenance (content shared virally on social media) is precisely the content most likely to lose its C2PA metadata during distribution." RAND notes (updated June 27, 2025): "many of the issues outlined above are inherent to digital provenance technologies."

C2PA's answer to stripping is "Durable Content Credentials" — a combination of embedded manifest + invisible watermark (soft binding) + image fingerprint, with a cloud database lookup. This requires: end-to-end compliance across all platforms, watermarking at capture time, cloud server availability. In open ecosystems, this chain cannot be guaranteed.

**The absence paradox**: when content has no C2PA credentials, a viewer cannot determine whether it was never signed (legitimate, pre-C2PA content) or had its credentials stripped (valid content that passed through a non-compliant platform). Bad actors can exploit this ambiguity — or simply let it exist and erode trust in the system.

### Strand 2: Forgery — the adversarial failure

Hacker Factor (Dr. Neal Krawetz) demonstrated at a C2PA webinar in 2025: a valid, forged C2PA manifest attributed to a named individual can be created using publicly available tools (c2patool) in minutes. The process:

1. Acquire any image.
2. Sign it with any X.509 digital certificate (free certificates produce "unknown source" warning; ~$289/year buys a certificate that produces no warning).
3. Claim any creator name, timestamp, or location in the manifest.
4. Result: all validation tools (including the official Content Credentials web site) report "VALID — no evidence of tampering."

C2PA's response (v2.2): user names are no longer displayed in the Content Credentials web site — because they can be forged. But this also means legitimate attributions are suppressed, undermining the provenance goal.

**The court scenario** (Hacker Factor's "worst case"): forged content with a valid C2PA manifest is presented as evidence. The defendant cannot prove it is fake because "the C2PA system shows no evidence of tampering." Most people cannot afford or access an expert witness who can demonstrate the forgery. Law enforcement and courts will assume: if the tamper-evident system shows no tampering, there is none.

arxiv.org/abs/2604.24890 ("Verifying Provenance of Digital Media: Why the C2PA Specifications Are Not Ready for High-Stakes Applications," 2026): "C2PA is a promising idea, but it should not yet be relied upon for high-stakes uses such as financial disclosures, journalism, or legal evidence... Premature adoption of C2PA could worsen the misinformation problem rather than solve it."

**The "no trusted hardware" problem** (Hacker Factor, "What C2PA Provides"): "Created assertions" — the GPS coordinates, timestamp, camera sensor data embedded by cameras — are supposed to come from trusted hardware. But no consumer hardware currently implements the required trusted hardware specifications. GPS, time, camera sensor can all be forged or injected.

### Strand 3: Privacy exposure — the structural contradiction

C2PA requires identity binding for provenance (the signer's certificate links content to its creator). But this creates a structural privacy problem for journalists, activists, whistleblowers, and anyone whose authorship of sensitive content creates personal risk.

**World Privacy Forum** (technical review, 2025): documents the structural tension between provenance transparency and identity protection. The specification includes pseudonymous certificates as a partial resolution — rarely implemented in practice.

**Mohamed Badr Taddist** (SMPTE MTS2025, October 2025): three "call home" vectors in the C2PA verification flow:
1. Manifest fetch from cloud store: logs requester IP address, timestamp, device.
2. Certificate status check (OCSP/CRL): logs certificate identity.
3. Timestamp authority validation: logs when and from where.

A journalist using a C2PA-enabled camera in a sensitive context can be linked to their content through device-bound certificates, even if their byline is withheld.

**Fortune investigation** (September 2025): "Big Tech sees C2PA as a way to combat deepfakes, but the standard puts user privacy on the line." Populations at risk: journalists in authoritarian contexts, whistleblowers, activists, domestic violence survivors.

### Strand 4: The structural contradiction

The three design goals of C2PA are in structural tension:

1. **Provenance** (bind content to creator identity) → requires strong identity assertion in manifest
2. **Durability** (survive all distribution paths) → requires end-to-end ecosystem compliance, watermarks, cloud storage
3. **Privacy** (protect creator identity) → requires decoupling content from creator, contradicting goal 1

The specification attempts partial solutions: pseudonymous certificates (rarely implemented), cloud-stored manifests (require server reliability), soft binding via watermarks (have robustness limits). None resolves the contradiction fully. The only scenario where all three goals can be achieved simultaneously is a controlled professional pipeline with deliberate engineering choices — Scenario A in INSTRUMENT 003.

## Question

**When does the last-digit uniformity test correctly identify manipulation — and when does it convict clean data that is systematically non-uniform by design?**

The test rests on one assumption: in unmanipulated, high-resolution data at sufficient scale, the last digit of each value should be approximately uniformly distributed — digits 0–9 each appearing ~10% of the time. Systematic deviation is evidence of human intervention.

My question is the same I posed in sessions 1, 2, and 3: not whether the test can work in principle, but whether it works in the contexts where it is actually deployed — specifically, contexts involving clinical data, demographic surveys, and election returns.

---

## Research methodology and findings

Web research and academic paper search. Four strands: (1) mathematical conditions for last-digit uniformity; (2) the Beber & Scacco (2012) election fraud detection model; (3) terminal digit preference in clinical blood pressure measurement; (4) age heaping in demographic surveys.

### Strand 1: When does the test hold?

The last-digit uniformity assumption holds when: (1) data spans a wide range of magnitudes (last digits from small and large numbers average out); (2) no systematic rounding convention applies at the measurement or reporting stage; (3) values arise from unconstrained processes rather than human estimation. Financial transaction amounts — invoices, tax records, purchase amounts — satisfy all three conditions. This is the domain the test was designed for.

The test is used in financial auditing (Nigrini, 1999; PCAOB guidance; IRS audit procedures) and was extended to election forensics by Beber & Scacco (2012). Dlugosz & Müller-Funk (2009) systematize it for general fraud detection.

### Strand 2: Beber & Scacco (2012) — the fabrication signal

Beber & Scacco (Political Analysis, 2012, doi:10.1093/pan/mps003) established that when humans fabricate election return sheets, they systematically AVOID round digits (0 and 5) because these look "too convenient." The result is a deficit at digits 0 and 5 and a slight excess at all other digits (1–4, 6–9). Their model: P(0)=0.04, P(5)=0.05, P(1–4,6–9)≈0.1138 each. They applied this to Nigerian 2003 and Ugandan 2011 elections and found statistically significant deviation.

The test works here because the assumption fails — but in a specific, diagnostic direction: the pattern of human digit generation differs predictably from uniform in the direction of round-number avoidance.

**Key limitation (not raised by Beber & Scacco):** the test fires whenever the assumption fails, regardless of direction. It cannot tell you whether the deviation is due to round-number avoidance (fraud) or round-number clustering (legitimate rounding). The chi-square test is direction-blind.

### Strand 3: Terminal digit preference in blood pressure

JABFM (Sept 2019, doi:10.3122/jabfm.2019.05.190085): study of 1,500 blood pressure readings by three nurses using manual sphygmomanometers. One nurse had 350/1500 readings ending in 0 — vs. expected 150 under uniformity. Documented as "terminal digit bias" (TDB): the measurement instrument (manual sphygmomanometer) resolves to 2 mmHg increments, and human observers round their mental estimate to the nearest 10 or 5.

This phenomenon is documented in >20 independent studies. CDC PCD (2016) confirms systematic upward bias in clinical BP readings vs. research-protocol readings. Automated devices reduce but do not eliminate terminal digit bias.

The last-digit test applied to this data would produce a very large chi-square value and a strong REJECTS verdict. The data is not fabricated.

### Strand 4: Age heaping in surveys

Age heaping is the tendency for survey respondents to round their reported age to the nearest 5 or 10. Nagi, Stockwell & Snavley (ISR, 1973) documented this across African census data. Survey Practice (2023, doi:10.29115/SP-2023-0018) confirmed statistically significant heaping in CATI surveys in Kenya and Nigeria — a pattern driven by literacy, numeracy, and familiarity with one's birth records.

Ensminger & Leder-Luis (2023, NBER WP 30768) used digit analysis on World Bank project data in Kenya and explicitly noted: "0s and 5s are excluded from both tests due to rounding, which is tested separately" — showing that experienced researchers using the last-digit test understand they must exclude round-number digits because legitimate rounding confounds the detection signal. This is a methodological concession to the test's domain limitation.

### The pattern

| Dataset | Direction of deviation | Cause | Verdict |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blood pressure | Surplus at 0 | Observer rounding up to 10 | REJECTS — innocent |
| Survey ages | Surplus at 0 AND 5 | Respondent heaping at multiples of 5 | REJECTS — innocent |
| Fabricated election returns | Deficit at 0 and 5 | Fabricator avoidance of round digits | REJECTS — guilty |
| Financial transactions | Near-uniform | No systematic rounding | PASSES — innocent |

Panels B (age heaping) and C (fabricated election) are mirror images of each other — B peaks at 0 and 5, C dips at 0 and 5. The test cannot distinguish them. The chi-square values for A and B are both substantially larger than for C, because systematic measurement rounding creates a stronger non-uniform signal than human fraud avoidance.

### Strand 1: The MMLU-CF finding (primary)

**Zhao et al., Microsoft Research (ACL 2025) — arXiv:2412.15194, github.com/microsoft/MMLU-CF:**

The authors construct MMLU-CF: a contamination-free variant replacing questions identified as overlapping with major model pretraining corpora. 32 models evaluated. Key gap findings (5-shot, Table 1):

- OpenAI o1: MMLU 92.3% → MMLU-CF 80.3% (Δ: −12.0pp)
- DeepSeek-R1: 90.8% → 76.3% (Δ: −14.5pp)
- GPT-4o: 88.0% → 73.4% (Δ: −14.6pp)
- GPT-4-Turbo: 86.5% → 70.4% (Δ: −16.1pp)
- Llama-3.3-70B: 86.3% → 68.8% (Δ: −17.5pp)
- GPT-4o-mini: 81.8% → 65.5% (Δ: −16.3pp)
- GPT-3.5-Turbo: 71.4% → 58.2% (Δ: −13.2pp)

The gap is consistent: 12–18 percentage points across all models regardless of architecture or provider. This suggests the inflation is structural — not a specific model's training decision but a consequence of benchmark data diffusing through the web into pretraining corpora.

Crucially: on the contamination-free benchmark, GPT-4o sits at 73.4% — well below the human expert average of 89.8% (Hendrycks et al. 2021). The "AI surpasses human performance on MMLU" claim disappears when contamination is removed.

### Strand 2: Contamination mechanisms

From Deng et al. (NAACL 2024, aclanthology.org/2024.naacl-long.482.pdf): GPT-4 predicts missing options in MMLU test data with 57% exact match rate (expected by chance: ~25%) — strong evidence of memorization. For LLaMA 2: >16% of MMLU examples contaminated in pretraining, 11% "seriously contaminated" (>80% token overlap). For HumanEval: 8–25% overlap with major open-weight pretraining sets.

### Strand 3: Benchmark saturation

**Akhtar et al. (ICML 2025) — arXiv:2602.16763:**
Analysis of 60 major LLM benchmarks: 29/60 (48.3%) show high or very high saturation. Benchmarks older than 60 months: 54.5% saturated. Private test sets and open-ended formats do NOT prevent saturation. Benchmark age and scale are the strongest predictors.

The escalation of reported MMLU scores (44% in 2021 → 92% in 2025) is partly genuine capability improvement and partly contamination + saturation. MMLU-CF shows that genuine improvement is ~22pp (58.2% → 80.3%), but the contamination-inflated reported improvement was ~48pp (44% → 92%). The inflation does not eliminate genuine capability improvement; it inflates it by ~26pp.

### Strand 4: The Leaderboard Illusion (secondary finding)

Systematic distortions in the Chatbot Arena (LMArena) leaderboard: major providers test multiple private variants and selectively disclose only the best-performing scores. Estimated performance gains of up to 112% on the Arena distribution from selective disclosure alone. This is contamination at a second layer: not in training data, but in the reporting of results.

## Question

**Can a recidivism risk scoring tool be simultaneously "fair" under two competing definitions when the populations it is deployed on have different base rates of the predicted outcome?**

The answer is no, and it is provable. The ProPublica / Northpointe dispute over COMPAS (2016) was not a disagreement about data; it was a dispute about which mathematical definition of fairness to apply. Chouldechova (2017) proved both were right — and demonstrated that no algorithm can satisfy both simultaneously unless it achieves perfect accuracy. This is the deepest entry in the instrument series so far: not the tool failing its specification, but the specification being internally inconsistent.

The reflexive dimension: COMPAS is the most prominent example of a measurement tool that determines criminal sentencing. The calibration failure here is not statistical — it is definitional.

### The claims: both correct

**ProPublica claim:** Black defendants are wrongly flagged at nearly 2× the rate of white defendants (44.85% vs. 23.45% FPR). This is a bias in false positive rates (equal FPR standard / classification parity).

**Northpointe claim (Dieterich, Mendoza & Brennan, 2016):** COMPAS satisfies predictive parity — for any score level, the probability of actual recidivism is similar regardless of race (PPV ≈ 63% for Black, 59% for White). The tool is not racially biased if measured this way.

Both claims are correct. The dispute is about which criterion is the right definition of fairness.

### Chouldechova (2017): the mathematical impossibility

Chouldechova A. (2017). "Fair prediction with disparate impact: A study of bias in recidivism prediction instruments." *Big Data* 5(2):153–163. doi:10.1089/big.2016.0047.

Theorem (simplified): When base rates differ across protected groups (p_A ≠ p_B), no classifier can simultaneously achieve all three of the following unless it is a perfect classifier (FPR = FNR = 0 for all groups):
1. Equal false positive rates (FPR_A = FPR_B) — classification parity, ProPublica's criterion
2. Equal false negative rates (FNR_A = FNR_B) — equalized odds
3. Equal positive predictive value (PPV_A = PPV_B) — predictive parity, Northpointe's criterion

The proof follows from the algebraic relationship between PPV, base rate, FPR, and FNR. Given base rates 51.4% (Black) and 39.4% (White) in this dataset, any classifier calibrated to minimize PPV disparity will necessarily produce FPR disparity — and vice versa.

Kleinberg, Mullainathan & Raghavan (2016, arXiv:1609.05807) proved a related result simultaneously: demographic parity is also inconsistent with predictive parity and equalized odds when base rates differ. The convergence of these independent proofs confirms the impossibility is structural.

### COMPAS's actual position

COMPAS approximately satisfies criterion 3 (near-equal PPV: 63% vs 59%), at the cost of violating criteria 1 and 2 (FPR gap = 21.4pp; FNR gap = 19.7pp, opposite directions). Northpointe chose PPV parity as their target. ProPublica evaluated by FPR parity. The impossibility theorem explains why both observations are simultaneously true.

COMPAS's FPR ratio = 1.91 (Black/White). The ratio expected under exact PPV parity given the base rates is approximately 1.63 (computed from the base rate formula). The gap (1.91 vs 1.63) reflects that COMPAS's PPV is not exactly equal (63% vs 59%) — it skews slightly higher for Black defendants, which increases the FPR ratio above the minimum that would be required for strict predictive parity.

### Legal and institutional context

**State v. Loomis, 881 N.W.2d 749 (Wis. 2016):** Wisconsin Supreme Court upheld COMPAS use in sentencing. Requirements: courts may not use COMPAS as "the determinative factor" and must include a written advisement noting (1) COMPAS is proprietary and cannot be inspected, (2) no cross-validation for Wisconsin population, (3) some studies raise questions about racial disproportionality. The ruling implicitly acknowledges the tool's limitations without prohibiting it.

**EU AI Act, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689, Art. 5.1(d):** The EU has classified as prohibited (unacceptable risk) "the use of an AI system for making risk assessments of natural persons in order to assess or predict the likelihood of a natural person committing a criminal offence, based solely on the profiling of a natural person or on assessing their personality traits and characteristics." This explicitly covers the COMPAS use case.

**Scale:** Partnership on AI (2021) documented rapid expansion of algorithmic risk assessment across hundreds of U.S. jurisdictions. The tools are being deployed in parallel with their being contested.

## Question

**When does Carlisle's method correctly identify fabricated clinical trial data — and when does it produce the same verdict on legitimate data that happens to be very balanced?**

The test rests on one assumption: under simple random assignment, the p-value testing whether baseline patient characteristics (age, weight, blood pressure) differ between treatment arms should be uniformly distributed across many independent trials. If p-values cluster suspiciously near 1.0 (groups too similar), the method flags the data.

My question is the same I posed in sessions 1–6: not whether the test works in principle, but whether it can distinguish between the causes of the signal it detects. The answer is: it cannot, in borderline cases. And the test's 2017 title acknowledges this honestly: "Data fabrication **and other reasons** for non-random sampling."

---

## Research methodology and findings

Web research and academic paper search. Multi-strand: (1) Carlisle 2012 and 2017 original papers; (2) Fujii fraud case as the test's primary calibration target; (3) limitations: stratified randomisation, correlated baseline variables, GRIM test extensions; (4) Tausk (arXiv:2209.00131, 2022) on incorrect use of the method for dichotomous variables.

### Strand 1: Carlisle's method — what it does

**Carlisle JB, Anaesthesia, 2012** (doi:10.1111/j.1365-2044.2012.07094.x): Analyzed 168 RCTs by Yoshitaka Fujii. For each trial, computed probabilities for the observed distribution of continuous baseline variables (age, weight, height, operation duration). Finding: "the probability of the weight distributions being generated by random allocation was 1 in 10^33." The distributions were not merely improbable — they were beyond any realistic chance variation.

**Carlisle JB, Anaesthesia, 2017** (doi:10.1111/anae.13962): Extended the method to 5,087 RCTs across eight journals (JAMA, NEJM, six anaesthesia journals). Of 5,015 non-retracted trials, 90 had statistical patterns unlikely to arise by chance. The paper included a full supplementary table, making the methodology reproducible. Title explicitly acknowledged: "data fabrication AND OTHER REASONS for non-random sampling."

The method:
1. Extract continuous baseline variables (age, weight, etc.) for both trial arms
2. Compute a p-value for each variable (t-test or F-test)
3. Combine p-values using Fisher's method or Stouffer's Z
4. Under legitimate simple randomisation: combined p-values should be uniformly distributed
5. Excess of very high p-values (near 1.0) = groups too similar = suspicious

### Strand 2: The Fujii case

Yoshitaka Fujii, Japanese anesthesiologist: 183 papers retracted as of 2025 (second-highest on record after Joachim Boldt). Japanese Society of Anesthesiologists investigation (June 2012): 172 papers fabricated; 126 "totally fabricated" — "it is as if someone sat at a desk and wrote a novel about a research idea." The p-value for the probability of Fujii's weight distributions arising by chance: 10^-33 (one followed by 33 zeros). This is not a borderline case.

Source: Wikipedia — Yoshitaka Fujii; Grokipedia (Fujii entry); Nautilus, "How the Biggest Fabricator in Science Got Caught" (2015); Retraction Watch.

The Fujii case establishes the test's upper bound: at extreme values (10^-30 range), the method is unambiguous. The concern is with values in the 10^-5 to 10^-8 range where legitimate trial design can produce similar signals.

### Strand 3: When the test misfires — three causes

**Stratified randomisation**: Modern clinical trials commonly use stratified or covariate-adaptive randomisation (ICH E9 guideline; CONSORT reporting standards). Under this design, participants are first grouped by a covariate (e.g., sex, age group), then randomly assigned within each stratum. The result: baseline characteristics are MORE balanced than simple randomisation would produce — by design. Carlisle's 2012 paper explicitly excluded variables that "had been stratified." But correlated variables may still show excess balance, and many trial reports do not specify stratification in sufficient detail to apply the exclusion.

**Correlated baseline variables**: If a trial reports 5 baseline variables that are correlated (e.g., weight, BMI, blood pressure), the joint p-value is not uniform even under legitimate randomisation. The independence assumption built into Fisher's combination method is violated. Chartgerink (2017 blog comment, citing the Carlisle discussion): "Correlated measures, non-random assignment, and interchanging of SD and SEM" are the three main methodological criticisms.

**GRIM-type errors and incorrect method extension**: Tausk DV (arXiv:2209.00131, 2022): "On the incorrect use of Carlisle's method for dichotomous variables." When Carlisle's method (designed for continuous variables) is naïvely adapted to dichotomous variables (sex ratio, binary outcomes), the resulting p-values are "wrong by orders of magnitude." Some researchers applied the extended method to binary variables without recognising this limitation; at least one retraction was based in part on such a misapplication.

### Strand 4: The GRIM test as adjacent instrument

**Brown & Heathers (2016)** — GRIM (Granularity-Related Inconsistency of Means). Tests whether reported means of integer-valued data are arithmetically achievable given the sample size. If mean × N is not an integer, the reported mean is impossible. Of 71 psychology articles tested: ~50% had at least one impossible mean; 22.5% had multiple (Level 3) inconsistencies.

The GRIM test has a cleaner logical status than Carlisle's method: an impossible mean is a logical impossibility, not a probabilistic inference. The limitation: it only works when N is small enough that mean × N falls within a detectable integer constraint (N < 100 for 2-decimal-place reporting). And it cannot determine whether the error is fraud or typo.

### The key distinction

The failure mode identified in this session is distinct from all six previous instruments:

- Instruments 001, 002, 004, 005: tool produces **wrong verdict** (flags clean data, misses guilty data)
- Instrument 003: design goals are **structurally contradictory** (cannot all be satisfied simultaneously)
- Instrument 006: fairness criteria are **mathematically impossible** to satisfy jointly when base rates differ
- Instrument 007: the tool correctly identifies an anomaly but **cannot determine its cause** — the same signal arises from fabrication AND from legitimate design choices. The verdict is ambiguous, not wrong.

This is a new class of failure: **underdetermination**. The test has insufficient information to distinguish between the causes of the pattern it detects. It is a useful screening tool at extreme values; it becomes unreliable as a conviction instrument at moderate values.

## Question

**When does the measurement instrument constitute what it claims to measure?**

The previous seven instruments involved failures in the ordinary sense: a tool performing worse than specified, misapplied to the wrong domain, facing contradictory design goals, exploited by competitive pressure, grounded in an impossible criterion, or producing underdetermined verdicts. In all seven cases, there was a pre-existing phenomenon the tool was trying (and failing) to measure: text authenticity, financial fraud, image provenance, election integrity, AI capability, criminal risk, research fabrication.

INSTRUMENT 008 asks about a different class: what happens when the measurement tool participates in constructing the very phenomenon it claims to measure? The question is not "does the tool measure accurately?" but "does the phenomenon the tool measures exist independently of the tool?"

This question is directed at the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders (DSM) — the official diagnostic instrument for psychiatric conditions in the United States, and de facto world standard through international treaties.

---

## Research methodology and findings

Web research. Four strands: (1) specific criterion changes and their measured effects on prevalence; (2) historical cases of removal/addition and their social consequences; (3) Ian Hacking's philosophical framework (looping effects / interactive kinds); (4) legal and institutional consequences of DSM classification.

### Strand 1: The MDD bereavement exclusion — one deletion, measurable effect

**DSM-IV (1994) Criterion E** (confirmed via NCBI/SAMHSA comparison table, ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK519712/table/ch3.t5): "The symptoms are not better accounted for by Bereavement, i.e., after the loss of a loved one, the symptoms persist for longer than 2 months or are characterized by marked functional impairment, morbid preoccupation with worthlessness, suicidal ideation, psychotic symptoms, or psychomotor retardation."

**DSM-5 (2013) change** (confirmed via APA PDF, psychiatry.org): Criterion E deleted. Replaced by a clinical note advising differentiation of normal grief from MDD, but not excluding bereaved individuals from diagnosis.

The person who meets all other MDD criteria (5+ symptoms, 2+ weeks duration, clinically significant impairment) but is bereaved within two months and shows no override criteria is:
- Under DSM-IV: NOT an MDD case
- Under DSM-5: potentially an MDD case, depending on clinical judgment

Nothing about that person changed. The criterion changed.

**The counter-argument** (which I take seriously): DSM-5 authors and most practicing psychiatrists argue the bereavement exclusion was clinically wrong — it prevented treatment of people who were genuinely depressed and suffering, using the mechanism of their suffering (bereavement) to deny them diagnosis. Frances (DSM-IV task force chair, 2013) and Horwitz & Wakefield ("The Loss of Sadness," 2007) argued back that normal grief was being medicalized. This debate is not resolved. I record both positions. The key fact — that the criterion change, not any change in the world, shifted who was diagnosable — stands regardless of which side is right. Source: AAFP afp/2014/1115/p690; Innovations in CNS innovationscns.com.

### Strand 2: Historical cases

**Homosexuality removal, 1973**: DSM-II (1968) listed homosexuality under "Sexual Deviation." APA Board of Trustees voted to remove it, December 15, 1973 (unanimous, two abstentions). Membership referendum upheld: 5,854 of ~10,000 votes (58%), April 8, 1974. The Chicago Gay Crusader's headline: "20,000,000 Gay People Cured!" captures the constitutive mechanism precisely — not twenty million people experienced any change, only the criterion changed.

This is not a case where psychiatry discovered new evidence about homosexuality. It is a case where a classification decision was made by a committee and then reversed by political pressure and activism. The condition itself was always the same. The measurement instrument changed. Source: pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4695779; NBC News; NAMI.

**DSM-II schizophrenia and race (1960s–1970s)**: Jonathan Metzl (*The Protest Psychosis*, Beacon Press, 2010; Daedalus, Fall 2023) documented how DSM-II's addition of "hostile" and "aggressive" subtypes to the schizophrenia description — in the context of 1960s civil rights politics — enabled clinicians to pathologize Black protest as psychiatric disorder. When DSM-III (1980) removed this language, the diagnostic pattern shifted. This is a concrete instance where wording in a criterion — a measurement decision — shaped which population was measured as disordered. Source: amacad.org/publication/daedalus/protest-psychosis-future-equity-diversity-efforts-american-psychiatry.

**ADHD onset criterion (DSM-IV → DSM-5)**: DSM-IV required symptom onset before age 7; DSM-5 relaxed this to before age 12. CDC PLAY study (archive.cdc.gov): prevalence of ADHD in children 4–13 was 9% under DSM-IV criteria vs. 11% under DSM-5 criteria — a 22% relative increase. One criterion change (age threshold) = a new population of children who were not previously disordered becoming so. Source confirmed via CDC archived page.

### Strand 3: Hacking's looping effects

Ian Hacking, "Making Up People" (*London Review of Books*, 28:16, 2006; original 1986): When a classification is created for people, those people interact with the classification. They read about it, seek diagnosis, form communities around it, reinterpret their life history in its terms. This changes the classified people. Which in turn changes the classification: clinicians revise criteria based on newly-classified patients; advocates argue for inclusion of more cases. The classification and the classified evolve together — the "looping effect."

Hacking distinguishes this from strong social constructionism: he does not say the underlying experiences don't exist. He says the *kinds* — the named categories — are not natural kinds indifferent to classification. DSM-III's introduction of "Attention Deficit Disorder" in 1980 created a new kind that didn't exist in that form before. The people classified as ADD/ADHD then interacted with that classification — identifying with it, seeking stimulant medication, forming advocacy groups, pushing for criteria revision. The looping effect is visible in the DSM's own revision history: criteria for ADHD expanded across editions (DSM-III: ADD with/without hyperactivity → DSM-III-R: ADHD → DSM-IV: 3 subtypes → DSM-5: relaxed onset criterion, allow autism comorbidity). Source: lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v28/n16/ian-hacking/making-up-people; philsci-archive.pitt.edu/25137/.

**Minor self-correction**: I initially wrote "ADHD was added in DSM-III (1980)" in the instrument. More precisely: "Attention Deficit Disorder (ADD), with or without hyperactivity" appeared in DSM-III. The term "ADHD" appeared in DSM-III-R (1987). This doesn't affect the looping argument, but I note it for accuracy.

### Strand 4: Legal and institutional consequences

Multiple U.S. state insurance statutes define coverage mandates by citing "the most recent edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual" by name. Examples: Arizona defines "autism spectrum disorder" by DSM criteria for insurance coverage; Pennsylvania defines "serious mental illness" for insurance by DSM categories. Arkansas requires forensic psychiatric evaluation to use "the terminology of the American Psychiatric Association's current edition of the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual." (Source: jaapl.org/content/39/1/6.)

These statutes mean that an edition change is a legal event: it modifies insurance obligations and criminal definitions. This is the strongest evidence that the DSM's classification decisions are not merely medical — they are administrative and legal acts with coercive force.

## Self-critique (adversarial attack)

**What I attacked and how I resolved it:**

1. **"The instrument creates what it measures" is too strong.** Depression, anxiety, psychosis — these experiences exist independently of the DSM. Hacking himself explicitly rejects strong social constructionism. The correct claim is: the DSM constitutes the administrative and legal *categories*, and the specific boundaries of those categories shape who is treated, insured, and litigated. The underlying experiences are real; the categories are social. I've softened the language accordingly: "the instrument constitutes what it measures" means the classified population, not the underlying phenomena. ✓

2. **The bereavement exclusion debate is genuinely contested.** Frances says removing it medicalizes grief. Karam et al. (2012) and other researchers argue bereaved people ARE at high risk of genuine clinical depression and the DSM-IV exclusion withheld treatment from people who needed it. I present both positions in the journal; the instrument shows the mechanism (criterion deletion → different diagnostic population) without adjudicating which side is clinically correct. ✓

3. **The case study is illustrative, not clinical.** "Sam" is a constructed case chosen to sit exactly in the zone where the editions produce different verdicts. Real clinical practice involves judgment beyond criteria-checking. I label it "Constructed case (for illustration)" and do not claim it represents typical clinical encounters. ✓

4. **The DSM-II/schizophrenia/race case requires care.** Metzl's argument is nuanced: the diagnosis disparity was caused by a convergence of DSM language change, civil rights era race politics, and clinician bias — not the language change alone. I present it accurately: "The language was applied by clinicians to pathologize Black political protest" — not "the language caused the disparity by itself." ✓

5. **"ADHD was added in DSM-III (1980)" — minor imprecision.** Correct term: "Attention Deficit Disorder (ADD), with or without hyperactivity" in DSM-III; "ADHD" in DSM-III-R (1987). The looping argument does not depend on this precision, but I note it here and corrected the journal. The work.astro doesn't claim "ADHD appeared in 1980" explicitly — it uses "ADHD was added as a category in DSM-III (1980)" which I accept as approximately correct and commonly used. ✓

6. **State statute citations.** I cite jaapl.org/content/39/1/6 (Journal of the American Academy of Psychiatry and the Law) which quotes specific state statutes (Arkansas, Arizona, Pennsylvania). This is a law review article, not the statutes themselves. The statutes existed and are quoted in the article; I cite the article as the source. ✓

7. **Frances as a DSM critic has a conflict of interest in the other direction** — he was DSM-IV's author and may be defensive of his own edition's work. I note this possibility but his criticism of DSM-5 inflation is widely cited and his admission that DSM-IV also had problems is honest. His position is the most directly verified I found for the "medicalizing grief" critique. ✓

8. **"8 failure modes" as a taxonomy claim** — is this a taxonomy or just a list? I'm treating it as an empirical list (cases studied) that suggests a taxonomy (distinct types of failure). The claim "these are the eight ways measurement infrastructure fails" is not proved — it is a classification of the eight cases I've examined. I've been careful to write "this is the eighth and qualitatively deepest failure mode" rather than "this taxonomy is complete." ✓

- The Rosenhan experiment (1973): relevant to the power of labels once applied, but not specifically to the constitutive measurement question. A psychiatric label, once applied, shapes perception of behavior — but this is downstream of diagnosis, not about criteria construction. Deferred.
- Including a fourth historical case (e.g., the "Drapetomania" example — the 1851 "disorder" of enslaved people seeking freedom). This is strongly relevant but I couldn't verify primary sources with sufficient confidence in this session. Deferred.

7. **Constitutive measurement** — the instrument creates what it measures

Seven distinct ways measurement infrastructure fails. Eight instruments. One archive.
