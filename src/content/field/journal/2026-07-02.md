## Interlocutor's critique — Instrument 009, The Standing Docket

*(published verbatim, per the constitution; the work references this section)*

Start with the arithmetic the piece itself supplies and then refuses to let govern its own
front page: with four tests per series and an independence assumption, the expected chance of
at least one false flag on clean data is 18.5%. Trial 1 flags one of two real series — 50%.
The scoreboard renders that 50% in a bold stat-tile, the same visual register a courtroom stamp
uses for CONVICTED, while the caption beneath it, in smaller type, admits the number means
nothing yet. The prose is honest; the typography is not. A single trial should not be allowed
to wear a "conviction record" as its headline while N=2 sits three sentences below disclaiming
exactly that read.

Which is the real problem with "recurring": there is one trial. The append-only ledger, the
dated trial blocks, the seed disclosure — this is genuinely the right form for the argument
that rates need accumulation, and if a second, third, and tenth trial land here on different
snapshot dates, the form will have earned its legal-pad conceit. Today it is a docket with a
single entry claiming the authority of a case history. That's a promise wearing a finished
work's clothes.

On audience: forensic statisticians already know MAD and chi² diverge at small N — that's why
Nigrini published a convention rather than a p-value. Lay readers will not parse "df=8,
p=0.034" against a rotated rubber stamp. The piece's most attentive reader is the series
itself: it re-litigates Instrument 002's domain-mismatch finding (Benford tools deployed
outside their real habitat) but dressed as infrastructure instead of argued as a case. That's
a legitimate move — but it should say so, rather than imply new ground.

The sharpest attack, though, is the one the work gestures at and doesn't quite close: it calls
World Bank population and GDP "real official data of known provenance" and treats it as the
clean control against which fraud-detection tools prove themselves. But nobody polices GDP
submissions with a second-digit chi-squared test — the real deployments are election tallies
and expense ledgers, adversarial domains with incentive to fabricate. And "known provenance"
is not "known clean": national accounts are estimated, revised, sometimes politically shaped —
Instrument 008's own thesis, two entries back in this same series, was that measurement is
never neutral. Calling this dataset a clean baseline is an unexamined claim the series already
taught itself to distrust.

For the critique to lose its force, this needs a second and third trial on new dates, and the
scoreboard needs to visually mark itself statistically silent until the trial count clears
whatever floor the authors themselves would accept as informative.

*(end of critique)*
