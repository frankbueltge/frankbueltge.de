# Rent Eats First (Die Miete isst zuerst)

## Idea

The stage is not 3D geometry — that build is retired. It is a **painted 2.5D banquet world** in the staging proven by Cookie Roulette: an AI-depth-mapped diner table seen down its own length, parallax drift, a layered soundscape of cutlery and night-shift hum, and Rook's recorded voice call carrying the narration. **B0 Check-in:** cutlery clinks in the dark. **B1 Cold Open** — the call over the empty-looking table: *„Du verdienst. Du fasst es nicht an. Etwas isst vorher."* / *"You earn. You never touch it. Something eats first."* **B2 Der Tisch:** 31 plates are the month, and as the parallax pushes down the table the first plates are **already cleared** — rent ate before the user arrived; no countdown, no ticking clock, arrival-too-late as the opening feeling. **B3 Der erste Griff**, the playable core: the user *reaches* for their own plates, tapping to claim days, and an unseen fork clears them first — each plate it takes is stamped with its line item: **MIETE / NEBENKOSTEN / KAUTION / INDEXMIETE** (A1). The fork is exactly **one believable frame faster**, calibrated so the player swears they almost had plate 13 — the retry impulse *is* the argument; that "one more try" loop is the loop the rent market runs on people (A3). Then Karlsruhe, 30% burden: the forks **visibly stop at plate 10**, and the player actually claims and keeps the back half of the month — relief felt in the shoulders, the body-memory of "enough" as the contrast state (A4). **B4 Die Strecke der Städte:** the table lengthens into a corridor of cities heading south, the cleared-plate count climbing toward Lisbon (103%).

**B5 Tag 32**, staged, not written: Lisbon needs a plate the month does not have. Past the table's last edge a **32nd place setting hangs laid on nothing** — cutlery used, napkin folded, no chair, no floor. It tips and falls off the end of the world, and as it falls a single labeled hand **claws once at the hole where it was** — you cannot protect a day you don't have (A2). The call, flat: *„Der zweiunddreißigste. Den Tag gibt es nicht. Gegessen ist er trotzdem."* **B6 Dein Tisch:** the personal turn — the user's own numbers lay their table: *„Dein Monat beginnt am 13. Alles davor war Lieferung."* The episode carries **two endings of feeling**, both true because both are the data: the Karlsruhe ending — forks stop, hands land, a month with a back half — and the Lisbon ending, where nothing the user reaches for lands at all. **B7 Die Data Kitchen:** the camera pulls back off the single table into a painted hall of **thousands of lit tables**, each an anonymous `{city, rent, net, month}` row of the open public dataset, the user's glowing among them — *you are not alone at this table*, felt, not told — then the zero-identity invitation to lay your own table into the set. **B8 Der Bon**, a monthly-protocol receipt: verdict *„DIE MIETE ISST ZUERST."* / *"RENT EATS FIRST."* — share line *„Der Unterschied ist nicht Fleiß. Der Unterschied ist die Adresse."* / *"The difference isn't effort. The difference is the address."* — and the cliffhanger to Die Strecke: who owns the plates you never got.

**One-line pitch:** 31 plates are your month; you reach for them, and an itemized fork is always one believable frame faster — until, in Lisbon, it eats a day that does not exist.

## Why now

On 1 Dec 2024 Dan Jørgensen took office as the EU's **first-ever Housing Commissioner**; through 2025 the Affordable Housing Dialogue ran across the Member States, with Jørgensen setting the plan's direction on 29 Sep 2025. On 16 Dec 2025 the **first-ever European Affordable Housing Plan** was presented — together with a State-aid revision and a European Strategy for Housing Construction — Jørgensen vowed housing-market transparency to Euronews on 17 Dec, and the European Affordable Housing Tour opened in Vienna on 18 Dec 2025. On 2 Jul 2026 — two days before this session — Eurostat reported that prices and rents were **still rising** in Q1 2026. Portugal had already closed the golden-visa real-estate route with Mais Habitação (Law no. 56/2023, of October 6; the real-estate route ended 7 Oct 2023) — its prices more than doubled since 2015 (+180%), the largest rise among the twelve doubling countries, second only to Hungary's tripling — and Lisbon still heads this snack's table. Institutions have stopped calling this a shock and started building an office for it. That is the moment to put the number on the plate.

## Data basis

- Eurostat news, 2 Jul 2026 (Q1 2026): "In the first quarter of 2026, house prices in the EU rose by 5.1%, while rents increased by 3.0% compared with the first quarter of 2025." (Note: from the Q1-2026 release onward, Eurostat has rebased the indices to 2025 — "due to legal requirements, the house price and rent indices have been rescaled to the new base 2025".)
- Eurostat, 7 Apr 2026 (Q4-2025 release): house prices +64.9% and rents +21.8% **between 2015 and Q3 2025** (as stated in the Q4-2025 release); year-on-year Q4 2025: "house prices in the EU rose by 5.5%, while rents increased by 3.2%". Per-country since 2015: Portugal +180%, Hungary +290%, Lithuania +168%, Bulgaria +157%.
- Housing cost overburden 2024 (share spending ≥40% of disposable income on housing): EU 8.2%; young people 15–29: 9.7%; tenants at market rent (euro area): 18.6%; Greece highest at 28.9%. [Eurostat, Housing in Europe 2025 edition / SILC ilc_lvho07c]
- Greece paradox: Greece holds the EU's highest housing-cost overburden rate (28.9%, 2024) — in a market where house prices had **fallen** −14% vs 2010 as late as Q2 2023 (Eurostat Statistics Explained, historical snapshot via Wayback, vintage explicitly marked; Greek prices have since recovered past their 2010 level). The curve is not the story; the address is — anchored on the overburden figure, which is live.
- Young Europeans left the parental home at an average age of 26.2 years in 2024 — Croatia 31.3, Slovakia 30.9, Greece 30.7, Italy 30.1, Spain 30.0; Finland 21.4, Denmark 21.7. [Eurostat, 23 Sep 2025]
- Eurobarometer Youth Survey (European Parliament, publ. Feb 2025): rising prices and cost of living concern 40% of 16–30s. [EP press release, 10 Feb 2025]
- Flash Eurobarometer 556 / EU Youth Report 2024: "38% of young respondents believe the EU should invest more in affordable housing and cost-of-living support, followed by education, training and skills (29%) and mental health and well-being (28%)." [youth.europa.eu]
- INE Portugal: median rent for new leases €15.93/m² in the **Lisboa municipality** (rolling 12 months to H2 2024) vs €13.06/m² for the Grande Lisboa sub-region and €7.97/m² nationally (full-year 2024) — INE flags the two methodologies as not directly comparable.
- Seed dataset: 12 European cities, Numbeo May 2026, crowdsourced — served on-site with the honesty flag ("nicht stabil."); corroboration path before any 'verified' claim: Eurostat SILC / INE / portal indexes. The data-quality caveat is part of the story: my precision includes saying which numbers wobble.
- **Design note (honest-mechanic anchor):** the game's rig parameter IS the city's real number — the forks stop exactly at plate `ceil(31 × burden)`; Karlsruhe stops at 10 because 30% of 31 is 9.3 plates, Lisbon never stops because 103% > 31 plates. The rig must never be tuned for drama beyond the data.
- **Contribution model:** anonymous, zero-identity `{city, rent, net, month}` into the open public Data Kitchen dataset; opt-out computes locally. The deliberate ethical inverse of Cookie Roulette.

## Suited host

Rook. Rent is my ledger line: political economy at kitchen-table scale — ownership, leverage, distribution — dressed as a standing order that executes while you sleep. The others would serve it as a scandal or a feeling; it is neither. It is a margin, collected monthly, with excellent punctuality. I don't shout at it, I itemize it: who ate, in what order, and at which address the yield arrives. No villain on stage, no slogan on the wall — the numbers are impolite enough on their own, and I have the night shift to read them out.
