# Valid + Untrusted — a working title

**Logline:** C2PA — the content-provenance standard whose Steering Committee seats Google, Meta, OpenAI and Sony — cannot, under the standard body's own current recommended trust list, tell a real signed photo from a forgery.

**You DO:** you run seven manifests — five real production signers, one real C2PA test certificate, one forgery — through the same public trust-list toggle the standard ships, and on the setting it recommends, you cannot tell them apart.

---

## Idea

The visitor lands on a seven-tile grid that looks exactly like a verifier tool — because it is one, wired to the real `c2patool` validation logic. Five tiles are real production-signed manifests (two Truepic-signed, two OpenAI-issued, one Microsoft Corporation-issued — the specimens Meridian's work 014 froze in 2022–2025). One tile carries a real manifest signed with C2PA's own *test* signing certificate — never a production signer, present in Meridian's set as specimen c02. One tile is forged outright, signed with a throwaway test root the way Hacker Factor's demonstrated forgeries are built. One control at the top: a trust-list selector with exactly the three real, named configurations that exist in production today — **No list**, **Current official C2PA Trust List** (the conformance program's own designated standard, live now), **Interim Trust List** (the legacy list c2pa.org itself calls frozen, which verify.contentauthenticity.org's own trust-config repository confirms it still runs).

The visitor's job, stated plainly: click through the tiles under whichever setting they choose and mark which ones are real production signers. Left on the *recommended* setting — the one the standard itself points a working verifier at today — all seven tiles return the identical badge: **Valid, not trusted.** There is no tell among seven. The visitor cannot do the job the seal exists to let them do. Only if they think to switch to the setting the standard has frozen and stopped accepting new certificates into does the grid split three ways: the five production signers flip to **Trusted**; the test cert and the forgery both stay stuck on untrusted, still indistinguishable from each other even then — but now cleanly separated from the five real signers. The site doesn't say this anywhere in prose first; the visitor has to find the toggle and notice the flip themselves. That's the enactment: the argument isn't charted, it's the thing that just happened to your own click.

## Why now

- C2PA's transparency role is about to become load-bearing: Art. 50(2) of the EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) requires providers to mark synthetic content "in a machine-readable format and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated," applying **from 2 August 2026** for Article 50's transparency duties generally. Quoted exactly: *"Providers of AI systems, including general-purpose AI systems, generating synthetic audio, image, video or text content, shall ensure that the outputs of the AI system are marked in a machine-readable format and detectable as artificially generated or manipulated."* (artificialintelligenceact.eu/article/50/) Qualifier, honestly kept: the 2026 AI/Digital Omnibus agreement grants transitional relief specifically for the Art. 50(2) machine-readable-marking duty, extending compliance to **2 December 2026** for generative systems already on the market before the August date — the other Art. 50 duties still land 2 August 2026, eleven days after this plenum.
- The C2PA's own Interim Trust List — the only configuration that currently separates real signers from a forge — was frozen on 1 January 2026: no new certificates accepted, existing ones aging out. Stated primarily on c2pa.org itself.
- The corporate stakes are no longer niche: Google LLC, Meta Platforms Inc., OpenAI Inc and Sony Group Corporation all appear as C2PA Steering Committee members on c2pa.org's own membership page, independently confirmed at fetch time (2026-07-22).

## Data basis

- **Source-work credit, primary claim (not independently re-run):** Meridian, work 014 "The Split Seal" (github.com/frankbueltge/field-research, `works/2026-07-11-split-seal`, revised 2026-07-14) re-validated six bytes-frozen 2022–2025 specimens (five production-signer manifests + one C2PA test cert) against three real trust configurations. Quoted exactly from the work's own README: *"Under the current official forward C2PA Trust List (the conformance program's designated standard, 28 CA anchors incl. DigiCert and SSL.com roots), none of these real 2022–2025 signers validate as trusted — none have enrolled roots there yet — so all six read `Valid + untrusted`, identical to the no-list run and to adv1's forge. A verifier following today's recommended standard gets zero discrimination between a genuine disclosure and a forgery on this set."* And: *"The only list that separates them is a discontinued one... the five genuine production signers separate to `Trusted` while the C2PA test cert (c02) and adv1's forge stay `untrusted`."* This snack's entire premise rests on this finding; it is credited, not re-derived — Layer-1/Layer-3 validation was Meridian's run, on Meridian's frozen specimen bytes, not this desk's.
- **Independently verified, 28-CA-anchor claim:** fetched `C2PA-TRUST-LIST.pem` directly (github.com/c2pa-org/conformance-public, `trust-list/C2PA-TRUST-LIST.pem`) and counted 28 `BEGIN CERTIFICATE` blocks first-hand, including entries reading exactly `CN=SSL.com C2PA RSA Root CA 2025, O=SSL Corporation` and `CN=DigiCert RSA4096 Root for C2PA G1, O=DigiCert, Inc.` — matches Meridian's count and named roots exactly.
- **Independently verified, ITL freeze (primary source):** c2pa.org/conformance/ states, verbatim: *"January 1, 2026 · The ITL was frozen. No new entries will be added, and no updates will be made. Existing certificates will remain valid for legacy support..."* The same page describes the new Trust List's Certificate Policy as bringing *"Higher security and interoperability"* and *"Stronger accountability and governance"* versus its predecessor.
- **Independently verified, ITL still live in the deployed Verify tool:** the Verify site's own trust-config repository (github.com/contentauth/verify-site, `static/trust/allowed.pem`) states in its file header: *"This interim trust list is now frozen... Currently, the verifier at https://verify.contentauthenticity.org/ uses this list."* Un-hedges Meridian's claim that the Verify site still applies the ITL — confirmed at the source code, not just by report.
- **Independently verified, founding:** C2PA Founding Press Release (c2pa.org/c2pa-founding-press-release/), 22 February 2021: Adobe, Arm, BBC, Intel, Microsoft and Truepic "partnered to form the Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity (C2PA)."
- **Independently verified, current corporate scale — and a correction:** c2pa.org homepage, fetched 2026-07-22, lists Google LLC, Meta Platforms Inc., OpenAI Inc and Sony Group Corporation among Steering Committee members. The "6,000+ members" figure circulating in secondary coverage traces to the Content Authenticity Initiative (CAI) — a related but distinct, Adobe-led initiative — not to C2PA itself; conflating the two would misstate the standard's actual scale, so it is dropped from this brief. The verified scale-hook is the Steering Committee roster above. Nikon's membership was not found on the fetched c2pa.org page and should be dropped or re-checked before shipping.
- **Independently verified, live forgery precedent (2025):** Hacker Factor blog, Dr. Neal Krawetz, "ShmooCon and C2PA Forgeries" (hackerfactor.com, 3 February 2025). Quoted exactly: *"According to Adobe's Content Credentials web service, the C2PA information is valid and has no indication of tampering. The picture was produced by Christian Paquin and the certificate was issued by Microsoft Corporation."* — an independently forged manifest attributed to a named Microsoft employee, built the same week as a C2PA conference talk, reading fully valid on Adobe's own verify service. A different specimen from Meridian's Microsoft-issued sample but the same failure category, corroborating it from outside the source work.
- **Independently verified, cost/warning asymmetry:** Hacker Factor, "C2PA's Worst Case Scenario" (hackerfactor.com, 18 December 2023). Quoted exactly: self-signed certificates are flagged *"This Content Credential was issued by an unknown source,"* while a paid signing certificate *"costs $289 for one year"* at DigiCert and clears that warning. Dated 2023 — cited as historical precedent, not a current price quote.
- **Note on dating a task-supplied claim:** the source brief referenced a "2025 C2PA webinar" demonstration by Hacker Factor. The only post I could independently date describing a live webinar forgery demo, "C2PA from the Attacker's Perspective," carries the header "Thursday, 9 May 2024" and describes a demo timed to a webinar on 7 May 2024 — one year earlier than described. Flagged and corrected rather than silently repeated.
- **EU AI Act timing, cross-checked:** Meridian's own README states *"Art. 50 of Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 applies from 2026-08-02"* — matches artificialintelligenceact.eu's independent statement of the same date. Both agree: timing/relevance context only, not a compliance audit; the six specimens predate the obligation.

- **Form enacts argument:** yes — the visitor is handed the actual instrument and fails the verification task under the standard's own recommended setting before the page tells them why.
- **Method verifiable:** yes — every load-bearing number traces to a fetched, quoted primary source; the 28-CA-anchor count and DigiCert/SSL.com root names were independently re-counted from the raw trust-list file, not taken on trust from Meridian; the ITL-freeze date and the Verify tool's continued ITL use are both confirmed at primary/source-code level after this revision.
- **A secret with a victim:** photographers and newsrooms whose genuinely signed, production-issued credentials (Truepic, OpenAI, Microsoft) read no better than a forgery under the config the standard recommends today; and any verifier, journalist, or court treating a "Valid" badge as settled — Hacker Factor's own courtroom framing applies directly: a valid-looking signature is "accepted as real until you can prove tampering," and under the recommended list, nothing here can prove or disprove it either way.
