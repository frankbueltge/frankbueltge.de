# Research

**Measuring the present — data, code, and the infrastructures of record.**

*Artistic research. Measuring instruments, not visualisations. Whether this is art is for
others to decide.*

This is a practice of artistic research working with data, code, and technical infrastructure.
It examines the present using the same means by which the present is measured and administered —
and makes visible what, in the process, is counted, passed over, or left unsaid.

## What are the investigations?

An investigation is a running measuring instrument: it asks a verifiable question about the
world, names its sources, discloses its method, and records its results day by day in a
versioned archive. Four are running, gathered under **"The File of the Present"**:

- **The Protocol** — the planet's session minutes every night, from twelve open sources.
- **Half-Life** — how quickly public attention to major events decays.
- **Parallax** — what the language editions of the same disputed thing state, and what they
  leave unsaid.
- **The Policy** — what the market charges the present, as a running insurance policy.

## Why data?

Here, data is both material and subject. Material, because it presents the present in countable
form; subject, because every measurement contains a decision — what is counted and what is not,
from which source, at what cadence. Those decisions are not hidden but stated. Form follows the
matter, not the other way around; there is no decorative distortion.

## Why code and infrastructure?

A measurement is worth no more than its reproducibility. Code makes the procedure verifiable;
infrastructure makes it durable. The investigations run as scheduled processes, record their
results under version control, and declare what they consume and depend on — sources, compute,
dependencies. What an investigation requires is part of what it says. The infrastructure is not
a tool kept out of sight; it is part of the subject.

## Why artistic research?

Because the questions are neither purely technical nor purely scientific. The aim is not to
optimise a metric, but to make perceptible how measurement orders the present — and where it
reaches its limits. Research, here, means a verifiable question, a disclosed method, and a
result that is allowed to fail. Discarded attempts are not removed; they are kept as notes.

## What does "measuring instruments, not visualisations" mean?

A visualisation decorates a result; a measuring instrument produces one. The works are built as
instruments: they generate a reading that would not exist without them, and they record how it
comes about. Diagrams serve the legibility of the measurement, not effect; where a depiction
would only be pretty, it is absent. The works' text is generated deterministically from
templates — no language model writes it. Where a model is used, for instance to extract
individual claims, the procedure is disclosed and every claim is checkable against the source.

## What limits are acknowledged?

Each investigation carries a method sheet that names its limits — and these limits are part of
the work, not its fine print:

- **Sources are not neutral.** Several investigations rest on Western, English-language, or
  US-centric data; this narrows the view, and it is stated.
- **Proxies are not measures.** Wikipedia page views approximate attention; a price index
  reflects priced-in risk, not the cost of the catastrophe itself.
- **Machine extraction is fallible.** Where a model classifies statements, it can err; that is
  why the prompt is published and every cell is checkable.
- **Omission is not censorship; attention is not worth.** A missing statement may be brevity; a
  short half-life of attention does not measure the worth of a life.
- **Failures stay visible.** When a source fails, this is recorded plainly, not quietly patched
  over; there are no back-dated readings.

What emerges here does not comment on the present. It keeps its minutes — verifiable, with open
sources, and with an admission of what it cannot measure.
